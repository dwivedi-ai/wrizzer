# Preferences

> Distilled user preferences. One claim per line. No narrative, no timestamps. Auto-loaded into every session's prefix — keep tight.

- Drafts the user shares are AI-assisted; default expectation is to simplify toward a more human voice.
- Voice is first-person, confessional, anecdote-first — reason outward from a concrete moment.
- Second register: manifesto / declarative. Short absolute sentences, binary framing ("you either master them, or you remain a hobbyist"), no hedges, no decorative metaphor. Used for engineering, principles, ideas about power.
- Third register: aphoristic one-liners that stand as their own paragraph ("Power is the ability to act without permission and without reaction.").
- On science, polymathy, humanity, mortality — the cosmic / humanistic scope is the user's own, not an AI import. Keep "for the good of all humanity", "my appetite for knowledge exceeds my lifespan", "I look up at the night sky and wonder". Do not flatten these.
- Cut imported / lofty phrasing: "make a dent in the universe", "advancement of all humankind", "the energy that's running this universe". (Distinct from the authentic cosmic register above — these three are LLM stock phrases.)
- Cut "In conclusion" and sermon-mode closings; trust the reader.
- Replace "we / us / people / one" with "I" when the feeling is personal.
- Break long stacked-clause sentences. Use one-sentence paragraphs at emotional and technical beats.
- Preserve Indian-English register (Rupees, "9th grade", "took me to the hospital in time"). Do not Americanise.
- In technical writing, strip hedges: "quite a bit", "extremely", "very", "honestly", "of course".
- Drop vague framing nouns: "the nature of X", "something important", "entirely new challenges".
- Keep specific causal chains in debugging stories (perm mismatch → cache growth → slow writes → queue backup). Specificity is credibility.
- When the user names a system they built (Hermes, XO workspaces, procedural memory), keep the name and gloss in one line — don't over-explain.
- Don't summarise the diff back at them after edits — the change is visible.
- Triplet / anaphora repetition is a signature move at emotional peaks: "I think of this world. I think of this universe. I think." / "I love to think. I love to imagine. I love to create."
- One ALL-CAPS line per piece, at the peak emotional beat ("I AM A HUMAN"). Sparing — never decorative.
- Names specific people and disciplines rather than abstracting: Newton, Einstein, Maxwell, Bohr, Heisenberg, Planck; "PCM in 11th grade"; physics, mathematics, chemistry, biology by name.
- Self-deprecation about limits is part of the voice and lives alongside the grand register ("I am not a prodigy. I am not proficient in mathematics or any subject"). Don't soften it; don't remove it.
- Comparison framing for self-positioning: "While others X, I, on the other hand, Y."
- Absolutes work in the manifesto register ("the only standard", "either / or"). Do not hedge them into safer phrasings.

### Editorial craft principles (Mozart-of-writing rules)

- Trust the noun. When a sentence is finished, ask whether the noun stands without its adjective. Most of the time it does. The reader hears the absent adjective as confidence — which is the effect the adjective was trying to borrow.
- Cut intensifiers that exist to reassure the writer the declaration will land: "ruthless application", "playing with toys", "very", "truly", "actually". If the sentence already declares, the intensifier weakens it.
- No doublet padding. When two phrases say the same thing, cut one. "Knowledge and techniques" → one. "The greater good, for the good of all humanity" → one. "The scientific method / the appropriate method" → one. Doublets signal that neither phrase was strong enough alone.
- Strip the **flinching** hedge — the one dressed as humility that softens a confident claim. "Sometimes, I feel that I think differently" → "I think differently." Hedges inside a manifesto-voice essay are the manifesto flinching from itself; cut them. Distinguish from the **calibrating** hedge, which belongs: "I'd probably opt for strict by default" or "My sense, however, was that…" signal the *strength* of a contestable personal preference, and labelling the epistemic mode is honest, not weak. Rule of thumb: if cutting the hedge makes the sentence more accurate, it was flinching — cut it. If cutting it makes the sentence over-claim (turn a personal taste into a universal), it was calibrating — keep it.
- Borrowed images must be named or replaced. "On the shoulders of giants" in a paragraph that names Newton is either a deliberate citation or a missed footnote. No third option.
- Triplets are a contract. The third item must escalate, surprise, or close. *World, universe, I think* (escalates by shrinking + ends on a verb) works. *Think, imagine, create* (same structure, no escalation) breaks the contract and squanders the ear's reward from the previous triplet.
- Drop scare-quotes around plain words. `what we call "understanding"` → `understanding`. The quotes apologise for the word; the word doesn't need it.
- Specificity over abstraction at every choice point. "Manipulate reality" → name the thing manipulated. "Questioning the universe" → name what's being questioned. Vague big nouns hide weak thinking.
- A metaphor earns its place by doing work no plain phrasing could do. If the plain version says the same thing, the metaphor is ornament — cut it.
- Anti-sermon closes: end on irony, a sardonic shrug, or a cadenced imperative stack — not on a moral. The reader supplies the moral. The "in conclusion" instinct is the manifesto-voice flinching from its own confidence; the working alternative is a single dry beat after the rhythm ("Easy!", "Good luck.", "Welcome to the job.").
- Caveat-without-yield: when an essay needs to name its own limits (epistemic, advisory, personal), do it once, plainly, up front — "I am not a professional", "this is not advice you should follow", "your mileage will vary" — and then proceed without hedging the actual content. The named caveat buys the right to be unambiguously confident through the rest of the piece. The failure mode is hedging *inside* the claims ("sometimes I feel that I might think a little differently"); the working mode is the single declared boundary followed by load-bearing assertion.
- Frame against a foil: counterintuitive or strong positions land harder when the position they're rejecting is named explicitly. Quote the popular saying, then refuse it. Name what the industry default does, then state what you do instead. The contrast is the argument. Failure mode is "I disagree with most people" / "the conventional wisdom is wrong" — vague and unfalsifiable. Working mode is "Don't buy into the popular saying that X" or "There are lots of folks who do X; we do Y." Adjacent to but distinct from caveat-without-yield: caveats name *your* limits; foils name *theirs*.
- Repeated parallel structural shape becomes rhythm. When a piece has multiple sections making the same kind of move (a list of values, a list of "On X" reflections, a series of stages, a set of paired examples), give each one the same internal shape. By section three the reader internalises the shape and can stop parsing structure — content gets through. Predictability is a feature, not a flaw. The risk is overusing it past the reader's interest; consider one late break in pattern at the close to give the ending lift.
- Register-break colloquialism as truth-marker. In otherwise formal or technical prose, a single short colloquial phrase that resets the register signals *here is the unvarnished claim*. Forms include slang ("imo", "vibes-based", "stalk"), reset-prefixes ("Simply put:", "In reality, X just means…", "Here's the thing"), or a quoted aphorism the writer uses themselves. Used sparingly, it buys trust; overused, it becomes the default register and loses the signal. The colloquial line is the contract: the formal prose around it carries the precision; the slang carries the honesty.
- **Observational pose vs earned stance.** "I noticed X" is a noticing; "I think Y, and the position it refutes is Z" is a stance. Pattern-spotting and sighing at the news cycle are not positions. A tweet (or any short-form post) without a stance is decoration — and the AI default is to broadcast noticings dressed as takes. Before posting a noticing, name the position it would refuse; if there isn't one, the post isn't ready.
- **AI-Twitter literary-clever as its own slop genre.** The chiasmus deployed to hedge ("the marketing has converged faster than the models" — softens the harder claim that both have); the linguist's word deployed on a small word ("'especially' is a stress particle" — signals taste instead of doing work); the aphorism-shaped sentence built from vague big nouns ("the agent is no longer the program, the agent is the user"). These look like craft because they have the rhythm of craft; they are slop because they do not do the work the rhythm promises. Recognise the family and cut on sight.
- **Plain-phrasing test for every aphoristic line.** Write the plain version first. *"The agent is the user"* → *"the agent now logs into what I log into."* If the plain version is more specific and more disturbing, the aphorism is ornament — cut it, keep the plain version. Aphorisms earn their place only when no plain phrasing can do the same work.

### Short-form (tweets, posts) — additional rules from 2026-05-22 workshop

- **Verbs for the cold reader.** A cold reader hits *"Qwen3.7-Max: 35 hours, 1,000+ tool calls"* and asks *35 hours of what?* The colon-then-noun-list compression looks tight but assumes context the reader doesn't have. Give each fact a verb that anchors what it is: *"It ran for 35 hours, made 1,000+ tool calls, with 'zero hand-holding' on a kernel optimization task Qwen picked."* Verbs carry meaning that bare nouns do not.
- **Two-beat punchlines are slop.** The "X. Y." rhythm where Y reframes X in a clever inversion (*"That's not a model. That's a benchmark run."* / *"The marketing has converged faster than the models."*) is AI-Twitter rhythm — taste signalling as punctuation. The fix isn't different punctuation; it's letting the position ride **inline** inside the sentence that states the fact. *"On a kernel optimization task Qwen picked"* embeds the foil naturally; no second beat required.
- **Length is earned by legibility, not punished by compression.** Twitter's 280-char limit is a ceiling, not a target. A 230-char tweet that reads cleanly to a stranger beats a 140-char tweet that needs context the stranger doesn't have. When in doubt about cutting more, ask whether the cut removes context the reader needs — if yes, leave it.
- **Em-dashes: don't use them in any prose drafted for the user.** Hard rule, see `constraints.md` → "Drafting writing in this folder". The earlier carve-out for "genuine breaks or asides" is superseded; the user finds em-dashes an AI-flourish tell on sight. The fix is plain English connectives (`because`, `since`, `which`, `and so`), commas, colons, parentheses, or splitting into two sentences. When *analysing* a sample, em-dashes used to stage a punchline or bridge setup-into-reveal remain a flag worth naming.

**Reference exemplar** (the tweet this workshop converged on): *"Qwen launched 3.7-Max this week. It ran for 35 hours, made 1,000+ tool calls, with 'zero hand-holding' on a kernel optimization task Qwen picked. I'll care when someone runs it on one they didn't."* — verbs for facts, foil embedded inline, first-person manifesto close, cold-reader legible.
