## What

External sample #5: a long-form HOWTO / explainer essay on a multi-stage model of behavior change, framed around interventions for each stage. Self-help-adjacent genre with academic citations, written by an individual blogger with intermittent personal asides. A noticeably different register from sample 4 — quiet explainer instead of swaggering blog — but the user picked it, so the question is *what makes the quiet register work*.

## Why it mattered

First explainer/expository sample in the collection. The previous four samples all leaned on a confident first-person manifesto or anecdote-first voice. This piece earns trust through different moves — specificity in citation, plain prose with one earned metaphor, signposted multi-register structure. Useful as a counterpoint: confirms that the "human-feeling" patterns aren't tied to one register.

## How it went

### What lands

- **Open on the puzzle, not on throat-clearing.** "In theory, behavior change should be easy. At first glance, it seems like you control your behavior. So, if you desire different behavior, why doesn't your behavior change as instantly as your desire to change it?" Three short paragraphs, each a single sentence, that *set up the question the piece is going to answer*. No "I've been thinking a lot about…" preamble. No "behavior change is a topic that…" framing. The first paragraph is the hook; the third is the question; the fourth ("In short, lasting change…") is the thesis. Efficient.

- **One-sentence paragraphs at pivot and definitional beats.** Used repeatedly: "In short, lasting change of habitual behaviors is a multi-stage process." / "Shame is the sense that you yourself are a bad person." / "In the context of undesired behavior, shame blocks change." Each one stands alone because it's load-bearing — a definition, a thesis sentence, a turn in the argument. The same signature the user already uses in technical writing; now observed in a third independent author. This is a cross-author signature of high-craft prose.

- **Specificity in citation.** "In 1977, as psychologists James Prochaska and Carlo DiClemente were studying the behavior of smokers, they identified these stages." Names two researchers + the year + what they were studying. Beats "researchers have shown" by a country mile. The move is the same as the user's "Newton, Einstein, Maxwell, Bohr, Heisenberg, Planck" — name the people, name the discipline, name the moment.

- **One earned aphoristic metaphor per piece, surrounded by plain prose.** "Models that you can't measure are a compass without a needle." One sentence. Aphoristic. Concrete image. Earns its keep because the surrounding prose declines to reach for ornament — when this single metaphor appears, it lands. This is the inverse of the AI failure mode (stacked metaphors that compete for attention). Plain prose is the *budget* that lets one metaphor pop.

- **Specific-absurd example with numbers and a weird object.** "if you decide to limit non-essential spending to 200 dollars per month as part of a financial recovery plan, but then impulse buy a 100 dollar smart toaster after you've hit your limit, you will experience foreseeable regret." Names the budget, names the price, names the slightly-absurd object (smart toaster). The absurdity is what makes it memorable. A generic "if you bought something you regretted" sentence would slide off the page. Specificity-over-abstraction applied to examples.

- **Disclaimer-without-yield as opening epistemic frame.** "Disclaimer: This article is based on my survey of the scientific literature and my own lived experience. I am not a mental health professional, and if you are struggling… please immediately consult a qualified professional." Names exactly what the piece is and isn't, then proceeds. Same pattern as sample 4's "This is not really advice you should especially follow" — observed in two consecutive external samples; the move is **state the limit, do not soften the content**. Second observation → lifting to semantic.

- **Personal asides used sparingly, anchored, then exits.** "Speaking for myself, I can attest to the efficacy of loving-kindness meditation in its ability to increase my sense of compassion for others and for myself." / "In my own experience, loving-kindness meditation helps a great deal." / "I personally experienced some of the most transformative insights through short lectures on free will by [a philosopher]." Three sentences max, anchored to the abstract claim just made, then the piece returns to the explainer voice. This is the move the user wanted from autobiographical-essay edits but in *reverse* direction: in an expository piece, the personal aside is the seasoning. In an autobiographical piece, the abstract claim is the seasoning. Same craft, different ratio.

- **Define-your-terms before deploying them.** "For the purposes of this article, it's important to distinguish between the short term and the long term when it comes to behavior. In the short term, behavior refers to a specific action or inaction. In the long term, behavior refers to repeated actions or inactions — habitual behaviors." Pedagogical scaffolding. Reader-friendly. Beats burying the distinction in a parenthetical or assuming the reader will infer it.

- **Distinction-pair done cleanly, one sentence each.** "Shame is the sense that you yourself are a bad person. … Conversely, guilt is the understanding that you have done something wrong, rather than feeling that you are wrong. Do not allow guilt to metastasize into shame." Three short paragraphs do all the conceptual work: define shame, define guilt, prescribe the relationship. Note the one verb that's allowed to do lyrical work — *metastasize* — sitting amid plain prose. Same move as the single aphoristic metaphor.

- **Multi-register prose with each register signposted.** The piece alternates between explainer-default, first-person aside (signposted by "Speaking for myself", "In my own experience"), quoted source (the 12-step language blocks), and disclaimer ("Disclaimer:", "And, again, if you are dealing with…"). The reader always knows which mode they're in. The signposts are explicit short phrases — beats sliding silently from one voice to another.

- **Concrete written-out examples for abstract moves.** The implementation-intentions section gives five fully-written if/when-then examples ("When I brush my teeth, I will take my prescription medication.") instead of describing the format and leaving the reader to construct one. Same as naming the numbers and the toaster. Specificity over abstraction at every choice point.

- **Closing imperatives at the end of the Epilogue.** "What is the next small, manageable step you can take today? Start there, and let each atomic action build upon the last." Short, second-person, action-oriented. This is the part of the close that *works* — see the next section for what doesn't.

### What doesn't land (failure modes even in a piece the user likes)

- **The Epilogue's opening sentence is borderline coach-speak / sermon-mode.** "Understanding and navigating the stages of behavior change is a journey that requires patience, self-awareness, and persistence." Doublet padding ("patience, self-awareness, and persistence" is three synonymous abstract nouns where one specific verb would carry more), and the cliché "is a journey" earns nothing. Two sentences later: "Embrace each stage as an integral part of personal growth and set your compass always toward the next stage." Borrowed image (compass), abstract noun ("personal growth"), exhortative verb ("Embrace"). This is the register the user wants stripped from his own drafts.
- **Acknowledgements paragraph reaches for the LinkedIn register.** "I'd like to thank…for her contributions to this article, including her suggestion that…" — clean but performatively gracious. Survivable, but not the part to imitate.
- **Takeaway:** even pieces the user likes have weak patches. The lesson is that the *rest* of the piece is strong enough that the weak Epilogue doesn't pull down the trust. A reader carries the compass-without-needle line, the smart-toaster example, and the cleanly-defined shame/guilt distinction — not the epilogue. *Strong middles compensate for sermon-mode closes; the goal is to cut the sermon-mode anyway.*

### Cross-author patterns now confirmed (user's drafts + sample 4 + this piece — 3 independent observations)

- **One-sentence paragraphs at pivot/definitional beats.** Already in semantic; now a 3-observation pattern across three independent authors and three genres. Locked-in.
- **Specificity over abstraction** — named numbers, named years, named objects, named people. Already in semantic; reinforced.
- **First-person used judiciously, not constantly.** Already in semantic (the "replace we/us/people with I" rule); the inverse holds — in expository writing, the personal aside is the seasoning. Two faces of the same rule.

### Cross-author patterns *now* at 2 observations (lifting to semantic this session)

- **Caveat-without-yield / Disclaimer-without-yield.** Sample 4 opened with "This is not really advice you should especially follow"; this piece opens with "Disclaimer: I am not a mental health professional". Same craft move in two genres. State the limit, proceed without hedging. Worth promoting to a craft principle.

### Single-observation patterns held for next sample

- **One earned aphoristic metaphor per piece amid plain prose.** Sample 4 had functional metaphors ("wizards", "blackbox filter") rather than an aphoristic one; this piece had the compass-without-needle. Different flavours. Hold and watch for a second instance.
- **Specific-absurd example with numbers + weird object.** Only here. Hold.
- **Define-your-terms before deploying them.** Only here. Hold — may be genre-specific to expository writing.
- **Multi-register prose with signposted transitions.** Only here. Hold.
- **Sermon-mode-Epilogue-failure-mode coexisting with strong middle.** Useful annotation but not a craft principle to imitate.
- **Frame the position against a named cliché.** Only sample 4. Hold.
