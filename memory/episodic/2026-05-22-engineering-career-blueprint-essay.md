## What

External sample #4 (first non-user-authored sample in the collection): a personal blog essay by an engineer on how they become "competitive" inside a new company over the first few months. Career/strategy genre, casual blog register.

## Why it mattered

First sample written by someone other than the user — so patterns that show up here that *also* show up in the user's drafts are candidates for "general human-writing patterns" rather than "this user's voice". The piece is also a clean example of the register the user already aims at: confident, declarative, anecdote-anchored, anti-sermon.

## How it went

### What lands

- **Caveat-without-yield in paragraph two.** The author writes "This is not really advice you should especially follow: everyone's different and context matters, but there might be some approaches in there that are valuable to pick up." Notice the structure: name the limit explicitly, then push forward. Not a hedge — a pre-empted objection. Lets the rest of the piece be unambiguously confident. The user's own drafts hedge mid-claim ("Sometimes, I feel that I think differently") which we already flagged for cutting; this is what the legitimate alternative looks like.

- **Self-deprecation embedded inside ambition.** "I felt criminally under-skilled for the position. Still… I would end up being the person to ask… The truth is that the reason this happened wasn't that I was wrong I was under-skilled — most of the time, I was." Confesses weakness mid-flex. Same register as the user's "I am not a prodigy. I am not proficient in mathematics or any subject" — sample 2 of this pattern, so it's now a cross-author signature, not just the user's quirk.

- **Slang and shop-talk as truth-marker.** "imo", "vibes-based", "blackbox filter", "the 20% guys doing the 80% that matters", "stalk their PRs", "wizards of the company". The lowered register signals *I actually do this, this is not the LinkedIn version*. When the same author writes formally a few sentences later, the formal phrase is doing real work because the slang has bought the trust. Pattern: slang + technical-formality alternate, neither dominates.

- **Unflattering honesty stated, not apologised for.** "what matters to who and, as rude as that sounds, who matters." The "as rude as that sounds" is the acknowledgment; the sentence does not retreat from the claim. Compare to AI-default which would either delete the sentence or soften it to mush.

- **One earned anecdote per abstract section.** The "On building relationships" section is mostly procedural (join channels, read messages, map their network), then drops one specific anecdote (slow-seeded emoji that paid off many months later) that *proves* the procedural claim. The anecdote does work the abstract claim cannot. Strip it and the section is empty.

- **Procedural detail anchoring abstract claims.** "I join the same channels, I read every message they post, I stalk their PRs, I try to map out in which spaces they move, what people they interact with etc." Names the literal moves rather than abstracting to "I learn their style". Specificity-over-abstraction in action — and the slang ("stalk") inside the list keeps it from reading clinical.

- **Lists carry argument; prose connects them.** The bullets are the load-bearing skeleton ("being able to work on…", "having accumulated…", "being in a place where I can break…"). The prose around them does transitions and texture. Decorative bulleting (one-word fragments, parallel headings with no payload) is absent.

- **Quoting one's own internal monologue as a list.** "I just joined — I am a tool to be used. I must be serviceable and provide value." The author treats his own self-instructions as quotable. Reads honest because it's specific and ungenerous toward the self (calling oneself "a tool"). This is a sharper move than "my approach is to be serviceable" — first-person internal voice beats third-person summary.

- **Frame the position against a named cliché.** "Don't buy into the popular & negatively-connoted saying that 'if you do more, more work will come' unless that is something you want to avoid." Names the conventional wisdom explicitly, then rejects it. The piece earns its unconventional take by quoting and refusing the conventional one. Beats "I disagree with what most people think" by a mile.

- **Sardonic close as anti-sermon.** Final paragraph stacks imperatives — "do the job, do it well, do it fast, do it a lot and with heart, make real relationships" — then ends on an ironic shrug: "Rinse and repeat. Make sure to stop before you end up in 25 hours of meeting a week. Easy!" The "Easy!" undercuts the moralism of the imperative stack and trusts the reader to feel the joke. This is what *replaces* sermon-mode closings the user wants stripped from his own drafts. The reader is left with the cadence and the wink, not a lesson.

- **Imperative stack as cadence at the close.** "do the job, do it well, do it fast, do it a lot and with heart" — same family as the user's signature triplets ("I think of this world. I think of this universe. I think.") but with a different structural move: short imperatives, escalating coverage, then the punchline (the "Easy!"). The third/final beat is the surprise/escalation that earns the rhythm.

- **Section headers as load-bearing signposts, not throat-clearing.** "Pre-requisites and mindset", "On building relationships.", "Outcomes" — each header earns its section. Notice the trailing period on "On building relationships." — a small typographic tic that reads as a finished thought, not a label.

- **Em-dashes and parentheticals as thinking-on-the-page.** "(depending on the context)", "(which usually entails already having acquired a lot of trust!)", and frequent " — " for mid-sentence pivots. The prose breathes the way speech does; the parentheticals carry asides without breaking the main line.

### What's notable in absence

- No "In conclusion." No "To summarise." No moralising final paragraph that abstracts away from the argument.
- No doublet padding. When the author wants to name something twice he uses different words doing different work, not synonym-stacking.
- No decorative metaphors. The metaphors the piece uses ("wizards of the company", "blackbox filter", "fine-tune a filter") are functional shorthand for ideas that would otherwise take a paragraph.
- No stock cosmic phrases. The piece is grounded in the workplace; it does not reach for "the universe" or "humankind".
- Almost no intensifiers. "Enormous amount of times" appears once and stands out as one of the few places the prose reaches for emphasis through an adverb.

### Cross-author patterns confirmed (now appear in user's drafts AND this external sample)

- **Self-deprecation embedded inside ambition.** Both authors flex and deflate in the same paragraph.
- **Specificity over abstraction.** Both name the literal moves / objects / numbers rather than summarising.
- **Imperative-stack or triplet cadence at the close.** Both end on rhythm, not on moral.
- **Anecdote-first / concrete-scene-first reasoning outward.** Both anchor abstract claims to one specific scene.
- **First-person register doing the load-bearing work.** Both refuse "one / people / we" drift when the claim is personal.

### Single-observation patterns (hold for next sample to confirm before lifting to semantic)

- **Caveat-without-yield as an opening move.** Pre-empt the objection in one sentence, then proceed without hedging.
- **Frame the position against a named cliché.** Quote the conventional wisdom; reject it directly.
- **Slang + technical-formality alternation.** Slang signals authenticity; formality where the argument needs precision; neither dominates.
- **Quote your own internal monologue as a list of self-rules.** First-person imperatives addressed to oneself, treated as quotable.
- **Sardonic single-word close after an imperative stack.** ("Easy!") — substitutes for moralising; reader supplies the moral.
