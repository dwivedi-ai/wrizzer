## Steps

1. **Read the tweet for the audience, not just the content.** Ask who is reading this thread right now and whether they are XO's buyers (technical founders, dev teams, platform leads). A high-profile, thoughtful post from a big account is worth more engagement than a niche feature update.
2. **Call reply vs quote-tweet, with a one-line reason.** Defaults:
   - **Reply** when you are joining someone else's conversation to add a real observation or a genuine laugh, riding their reach. Warmer, lower risk, no implied co-sign. Best when your take builds on theirs rather than standing alone, and best for big posts where the replies get a lot of eyeballs.
   - **Quote-tweet** when you have a take you want on XO's own timeline in front of XO's followers, and the post gives you a clean co-sign to build on. The only home for any product mention.
3. **Find the honest hook.** Look for the detail most people will skim past that XO is genuinely positioned to comment on. Match it to what XO actually is (where agents run, scale, visibility), not to the cleverest-sounding thesis. If a post celebrates parallel/fan-out agents, the wedge is usually "your hardware is the ceiling."
4. **Draft value first.** Lead with a real insight or reaction. For a reply, that is the whole post (no pitch). For a QRT, the value beat earns a soft product line underneath, never the reverse.
5. **Run the voice check.** Gen-Z-fluent but B2B-credible. Concrete over abstract, no colon-compression, no em-dashes, no two-beat AI-Twitter punchlines, longer-and-human over tight-and-clipped. Would a CTO trust it and would a builder enjoy it.
6. **Offer the alternative format in one line** (e.g. "if you'd rather not co-sign on your own timeline, I'll convert to a reply") and a length trim, then let the user grade and post. wrizzer drafts; the user publishes.

## Gotchas

- Never pitch XO under someone else's tweet. Pitches live only in QRTs on XO's own timeline. See `constraints.md`.
- Don't engage with everything. Recommending "skip this one, agreement isn't worth a post" is a valid and valuable output.
- Re-anchor the angle once you know the product. The first instinct ("the harness is the moat") pointed at orchestration logic, which is not what XO sells; the real wedge was where agents run. Always sanity-check the co-sign against XO's actual positioning in `project-facts.md`.
- For "general / non-marketing" posts, plural is allowed (a small set to calibrate voice), but still have taste: every one should be a post you'd stand behind, not filler. Pick the strongest one or two and say so.
- **Trim before he has to.** When the user edits a draft before posting, his consistent cuts are: the framing on the front ("the part people will sleep on is..."), the clever button on the back ("Nice split", "and this is the bridge"), and any flourish that delays the point. Pre-trim these. State the good sentence plainly and stop. See the "What landed live" section in `preferences.md`.
- **Let enthusiasm in.** Drafts skew dry and analytical; the user happily adds genuine excitement ("This is so cool!"). For posts that are reactions to something genuinely cool, an earnest tone is on-voice, not a credibility risk.
