### Cross-author patterns now confirmed (3+ independent observations — locked-in)

- One-sentence paragraphs at pivot/definitional beats (5 observations)
- Named specificity over abstract claims (numbers, years, objects, people, languages)
- First-person used to declare preference without arguing for it
- Anti-sermon close — variously sardonic shrug, toast format, self-observation, or cadenced imperative stack; what unites them is *no moralising*

### Patterns under watch (single observation, awaiting second sample)

> Promote to the Mozart section of `memory/semantic/preferences.md` when a second independent sample shows any of these, and remove from this list. Patterns themselves are preserved in their source episode files in `memory/episodic/`.

**From sample 4 (career/strategy blog):**
- **Quote your own internal monologue as a list of self-rules.** First-person imperatives addressed to oneself, treated as quotable ("I just joined, I am a tool to be used.").
- **Sardonic single-word close after an imperative stack.** A dry beat ("Easy!") substituting for moralising; reader supplies the moral. *(Sub-form of the already-promoted anti-sermon close rule, but the specific shape is worth confirming.)*

**From sample 5 (pedagogical explainer):**
- **One earned aphoristic metaphor amid plain prose.** A single line of imagery ("a compass without a needle") that earns its keep because the surrounding prose declines to reach for ornament.
- **Specific-absurd example.** Concrete numbers paired with a slightly weird object (smart toaster); more memorable than a generic example.
- **Define-your-terms before deploying them.** Pedagogical scaffolding at the start of an expository piece.
- **Multi-register signposted prose.** Alternating between explainer-default / first-person aside / quoted source / disclaimer, each signposted by a short phrase so the reader knows which mode they're in.

**From sample 6 (founder letter + blog framing):**
- **Inverted-causation / chiasmus opening as thesis.** "We don't X to Y; we Y to X."
- **Definitional reclamation of a loaded word.** Name the bad connotation, then replace it with the working meaning.
- **Personal-voice parenthetical inside a financial figure.** "$X billion (!)", a wink that signals the writer is still surprised.
- **Toast-format admiration close.** "Here's to [X who did specific things]"; admiration via named actions, not abstract qualities.
- **Pair abstract value with operational paragraph.** Each value name gets specific behaviour or aphorism, never floats as a noun.

**From sample 7 (technical reflection on language design):**
- **Updated-belief move.** "That's a notable shift from my prior perspective, where I felt that X"; names the prior position and the update, builds credibility.
- **Counterfactual / conditional framing for opinions.** "Were I to design a language, I'd…"; lets the writer commit without claiming the authority to decide.
- **Synthesis-position stated as a stronger commitment than either pole.** Doesn't hedge into the middle; commits to it as the harder, more demanding position.
- **Interrogative / skeptical quotes around a single word.** `"faster"` programming language; quotes signal *I'm about to complicate this term*. Distinct from apologetic scare-quotes (already covered by the "drop scare-quotes around plain words" rule).
- **Mid-piece exception statement.** Like caveat-without-yield but mid-piece: "A notable exception was X"; names what breaks the writer's own pattern.

### What to look for in the next sample batch

- **Short-form sample (tweet / aphorism)** would stress the existing pattern set. Most current rules assume essay-length prose; hard to apply "one-sentence paragraph at definitional beat" to a piece that is *one* sentence.
- **Fiction sample** would likely shift the pattern landscape. Fiction's craft moves (scene, dialogue, sensory detail) don't map cleanly onto the current essay-oriented rules.
- **Marketing/copy sample** would test where the line is between "voice" and "manipulation". Copy lives by the same anti-vague-noun, anti-doublet rules, but also has structural constraints the current samples don't.

### Open questions

- External samples are user-driven (he picks); should the next batch be steered toward the missing genres, or remain user-driven? Currently happy to take what he sends.
- AI-style failure modes appearing *inside* pieces the user likes (sample 5 Epilogue, sample 6 acknowledgements) are annotated as failure-mode-coexisting-with-strong-middle. Useful by-product — worth surfacing in the eventual rubric.

### Risks / unknowns

- "Feels human" is slippery. Mitigated by the rule that every annotation must name a specific move, not an adjective.
- Sample bias: one author per sample so far, variance improving with each external. Aim for at least one short-form piece and one fiction sample in the next batch — both are structurally different enough to stress the existing pattern set.
